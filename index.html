Section 1

Current state → future state

🧭

Bo University is our learning management system — the platform where training lives, where employees complete courses, and where leaders track team progress. Right now it functions primarily as a content library. This plan describes how to make it a readiness and performance tool instead.

The bigger question is not “what training should live in the LMS?” It is “how should the LMS function as a readiness, enablement, and visibility system for different audiences?” This hub is built to answer that question — showing the current state, the future state, the roadmap, and the measurement model in one place, with a practical path to implementation by EOY.

Current state Storage

The LMS is experienced primarily as a place to store and complete training, with limited readiness, support, visibility, and governance clarity.

Future state Ecosystem

The LMS becomes a role-based readiness, enablement, governance, and visibility ecosystem.

Plan 3–6 Months

A phased implementation path with enough buffer to land the core operating model by EOY.

Success Measured

Success is judged through usability, readiness, engagement, and admin-efficiency signals.

Key terms used in this document

  • Readiness — Whether someone is actually prepared to do their job well: not just whether they completed a course, but whether they have the knowledge, tools, access, and support they need.
  • Enablement — The resources, job aids, and structures that help people apply what they’ve learned in the moment they need it.
  • Governance — The rules that keep the platform organized: who owns what, who can change what, and how content gets created, maintained, and retired.
  • Branches & Groups — How users are organized in the platform. Branches = market or location type (e.g. Core, Expansion). Groups = role (e.g. GM, Shift Leader). Together they control what each person sees.
  • Channels — Curated resource libraries inside the LMS — job aids, guides, videos organized by topic, accessible without enrolling in a course.

North Star

The LMS should function as a role-based readiness and enablement ecosystem that supports learning, onboarding, performance support, governance, and execution across the organization.

Role-Based Governed Transparent Measurable

EOY objective

  • Future-state structure defined and approved
  • Role-based user/admin experience in place
  • Governance and permissions model activated
  • Core readiness and enablement layer operational

Section 4

Operating model

🏗️

The architecture should be thought of in layers so the experience, structure, governance, and support logic can be designed intentionally rather than treated as one undifferentiated platform.

4.1 Audience + experience architecture One LMS, multiple front doors by role and purpose.
+

Primary audiences

  • Field learners and frontline operators
  • Store leaders and managers
  • Above-store leaders
  • Admins, content owners, and support teams

UX rule

Each audience should encounter different landing pages, priorities, dashboards, and support tools based on what they need to do — not simply what content is available.

4.2 Information + learning architecture Structure the LMS around action, readiness, and visibility.
+

Primary navigation buckets

Learn Readiness Perform Insights Support Governance

Design rule

Learning belongs inside a broader system of readiness tools, support pathways, dashboards, checklists, job aids, and governance references.

4.3 Permissions + data logic Make ownership, visibility, and access rules explicit.
+

Core questions

  • Who sees what?
  • Who manages what?
  • Who edits or approves what?
  • Who sees which dashboard views?

Outcome

A transparent governance model that reduces confusion, clarifies ownership, and improves trust in the system.

Section 5

Governance playbook overview

🏛️

Governance and transparency should work together. The system should be disciplined enough to scale and clear enough that people understand ownership, access, dashboard visibility, and how changes happen.

What governance should define

  • Who owns and maintains each system component
  • Who can edit, approve, publish, or archive content
  • How changes are requested and reviewed
  • How dashboard visibility and reporting access work
  • How governance is made transparent to stakeholders

Why it matters

Great governance is often invisible when it works well. Poor governance is highly visible. A strong governance playbook lowers confusion, reduces duplication, protects experience consistency, and makes ownership easier to sustain.

5.1 Decision rights + escalation model Governance is tested when there is disagreement, not when everything is easy.
+

What should be explicit

  • Who makes the final call on structure, taxonomy, and publishing rules
  • What gets escalated versus handled locally
  • Turnaround expectations for approvals and decisions
  • What qualifies as a governance issue versus a content issue

Why it matters

A governance model can feel strong until conflict appears. Clear decision rights and escalation triggers prevent stalled work, duplicate effort, and local exceptions that weaken the system.

5.2 Content lifecycle + archive rules Prevent the LMS from becoming cluttered through accumulation.
+

Lifecycle stages

Request Review Publish Maintain Evaluate Archive

Every course, resource, widget, and dashboard should have a visible lifecycle and review cadence.

Rules to define

  • How content is requested and reviewed
  • How outdated content is flagged
  • When resources are revised vs retired
  • How duplicates are prevented
5.3 Taxonomy, naming, and search strategy Discoverability depends on naming discipline and a deliberate search experience.
+

Taxonomy standards

  • Course naming conventions
  • Dashboard naming conventions
  • Tags and metadata rules
  • User-facing vs admin-facing labels

Search strategy

  • What users should be able to search by
  • How results should be prioritized
  • Which synonyms and aliases should be accounted for
  • How search failures should be monitored and corrected
5.4 Standardization, localization, and auditability Protect consistency while still allowing audience or regional nuance.
+

Standardization vs localization

  • Define what must stay standardized enterprise-wide
  • Clarify where branches, regions, or audiences may vary
  • Prevent uncontrolled customization

Risk, compliance, and auditability

  • Version control and change traceability
  • Training and certification integrity
  • Who changed what and when
  • Access control and reporting integrity

Section 7

Design system: UX, admin experience, and research-informed standards

🎨

The LMS should not only be well-governed and well-structured. It should also look and feel intentionally designed. A design system section makes the visual and interaction standards explicit so the experience is more usable for learners, more manageable for administrators, and more trustworthy across the platform.

What this section should define

  • Color palette and what each color means in the interface
  • Font system for hierarchy, readability, and consistency
  • Widget and card styles for dashboards, alerts, actions, and support tools
  • Spacing, page layout, and navigation patterns
  • Interaction rules for buttons, accordions, tabs, and expandable widgets
  • Standards for balancing user experience and admin experience

Why it matters

Design choices shape clarity, trust, discoverability, and cognitive load. When the aesthetic system is intentional, users find what they need faster, admins maintain the platform more consistently, and stakeholders can understand how the LMS is meant to function without guessing.

7.1 Visual system standards Define the aesthetic language that makes the LMS feel consistent, calm, readable, and credible.
+

Suggested standards

Navy / ink foundation Warm neutral background Gold accent for emphasis Readable contrast Clear visual hierarchy

Use darker ink tones for trust and structure, warm neutrals for readability, and a restrained accent color for emphasis, action, and signals. Avoid visual noise that makes the system feel cluttered or overly promotional.

Typography guidance

  • One primary sans-serif for interface clarity and readability
  • One serif or elevated heading font only if it supports hierarchy and brand tone
  • Consistent heading scale for pages, widgets, cards, and dashboards
  • Readable body text sizing for both desktop and mobile use
7.2 Widget and component strategy Make the design rules for cards, widgets, tiles, alerts, and dashboards visible.
+

Priority components

  • Role-based homepage tiles
  • Dashboard summary cards
  • Readiness or risk indicators
  • Support and resource widgets
  • Accordion sections for layered information
  • Quick-action buttons for common admin tasks

Design rule

Widgets should help people act, not just decorate the interface. Every component should answer one of these: what do I need to know, what do I need to do, where do I go next, or what requires attention?

7.3 Research-informed experience principles Connect the design standards to usability, cognitive load, and administrative efficiency.
+

User experience principles

  • Reduce cognitive load through clear grouping and hierarchy
  • Use predictable patterns so navigation becomes intuitive
  • Make required next steps obvious
  • Limit clutter and competing calls to action
  • Use consistent labels across pages, dashboards, and resources

Admin experience principles

  • Make maintenance pathways visible and repeatable
  • Reduce one-off design exceptions that create management burden
  • Use reusable page and widget patterns
  • Clarify edit rights and component ownership
  • Design for scalability, not just one strong page
Section 8

Readiness + enablement

🚀

This is the differentiator. Most LMS platforms measure whether someone completed a course. This architecture is built around a different question: is this person actually ready to do their job? Those are not the same thing.

The Readiness Model — Six Components

A person is ready to perform when all six of these are true — not just when they’ve completed a course. The LMS should help identify which component is missing when performance breaks down.

Knowledge Access Workflow Clarity Expectations Manager Support Tools + Resources

Example: a Shift Leader who completed all their training but still struggles may be missing Manager Support or Workflow Clarity — not knowledge. The LMS should surface that, not just record the completion.

Data collection opportunities

  • Readiness diagnostic submissions and support request categories
  • Resource gaps by role or workflow
  • Recurring friction themes that indicate a systems issue, not just a learning gap
Section 9

Measurement, analytics, and passive signal collection

📊

This section explains how success will be measured and how the LMS can generate passive intelligence without requiring users to do anything differently. The LMS should be instrumented to capture what people already do: what they click, open, revisit, search for, ignore, spend time with, abandon, and return to. That creates a passive signal layer that helps the organization understand engagement, readiness, friction, discoverability, and admin burden without relying only on surveys or self-report.

Guiding principle

Instrument what people already do before asking them for anything new. Use passive signals first, then layer in support intake, and then correlate usage patterns, friction, and ticket submissions to improve the system over time.

GA4 xAPI Dashboards Resource Effectiveness Passive Data

What this layer should answer

  • What are people gravitating toward?
  • What resources are never used or rarely opened?
  • What pages or courses are opened but not completed?
  • Where are users spending time versus dropping off?
  • Which support topics persist even when resources exist?
  • What is creating admin burden behind the scenes?
9.1 What can be measured passively Capture behavioral signals without changing the user workflow.
+

Platform and navigation signals

  • Page views and unique page viewers
  • Repeat visits and return behavior
  • Homepage widget clicks and tile clicks
  • Internal navigation paths
  • Search usage, terms, and search-to-resource clicks
  • Exit points and abandoned navigation paths

Resource and course signals

  • Unique resource viewers vs total views
  • Repeat opens of a resource
  • Launches, starts, completions, and abandonment
  • Average time spent and median time spent
  • Reopens or re-entry rate for a course or asset
  • Module-level drop-off for interactive learning content
9.2 Measurement stack Separate what is best measured by platform analytics, content instrumentation, and reporting.
+

GA4 / platform usage layer

  • Page views and unique visitors
  • Clicks on homepage modules and resource tiles
  • Navigation path analysis
  • Page-level time and engagement
  • Search and entry/exit behavior

xAPI / learning interaction layer

  • Detailed interactive content behavior
  • Tracked activities and sub-activities
  • Time within interactive objects
  • Completion, revisit, and engagement patterns
  • More granular data for high-value assets

Reporting / dashboard layer

Consolidate passive usage data, learning interaction data, and support signals into dashboards that help leaders and admins act. This layer is where page analytics, learning behavior, and support demand should come together.

Support + ticket correlation layer

Connect support-ticket topics, readiness diagnostic themes, and resource usage to understand whether a resource is invisible, ineffective, or missing entirely.

Section 10

Adoption, support model, and rollout

🤝

A strong architecture can still fail if people do not understand what changed, what is expected of them, or where to go for help. This section defines how the system is introduced, supported, and sustained after rollout.

Change management + adoption plan

  • How the new structure is communicated to stakeholders
  • How users are introduced to the new experience
  • How admins and content owners are trained
  • What expectations are set for leaders and managers
  • How adoption is monitored after launch

Support operating model

  • Technical issue
  • Access / permissions issue
  • Content issue
  • Workflow confusion
  • Readiness barrier
  • Resource request
  • Governance question

Each category should have a routing path, response expectation, and owner so support does not become a black box.

Section 11

MVP, pilot validation, and maturity model

🌱

A clear distinction between the MVP operating state and the ideal future state protects the EOY target. This section defines what must be true by launch, what should be piloted first, and what can mature afterward.

11.1 MVP by EOY What must be true for the launch to count as successful.
+

Minimum viable operating state

  • Role-based homepage or navigation logic is active
  • Core learning and readiness pathways are restructured
  • Permissions and governance model are in place
  • Primary dashboards and access rules are functioning
  • Support and enablement layer is visible in the LMS

Why this matters

Defining the MVP prevents the project from trying to perfect everything at once. It protects the launch while still leaving room for future maturity.

11.2 Pilot + validation strategy Test whether the architecture actually works before broadening it.
+

Pilot questions

  • Who pilots the future-state model first?
  • What parts of the design get piloted before broader launch?
  • What feedback is collected from users and admins?
  • What gets revised before expansion?

Validation focus

  • What users ignore
  • What admins still find painful
  • Where confusion remains
  • Whether dashboards actually trigger action
11.3 Future-state maturity model What can follow after the EOY launch.
+

Maturity stages

Phase 1 / MVP Phase 2 / Enhanced Experience Phase 3 / Advanced Analytics Phase 4 / Optimization + Scale

Strategic benefit

A maturity model gives the organization a realistic path forward without turning every future-state idea into a launch requirement.

          14.1 Current State Diagnosis What was observed in the platform and what it means architecturally.
          +

          Key finding

          These are structural problems, not content problems. 267 courses, 138 catalogs, 30 pages, 5 menus, 0 active Channels. The promotional homepage, flat course grid, and catalog sprawl are all downstream of missing governance and role architecture.

          What was observed

          • Homepage: Hero banner + Bo Kind feature + category scroll — same for all roles. No role differentiation, no readiness framing.
          • My Courses: 78+ items, flat card grid, sorted by newest enrollment. LTOs mixed with leadership content, no path hierarchy.
          • Catalogs: 138 catalogs across 7 pages — franchise codes, LTO-specific, test catalogs. No naming convention, no ownership.
          • Channels: 0 of 0 active. No enablement resource layer anywhere in the platform.
          • Menus: 5 menus — no Crew menu, no Shift Leader menu, no AD/RDO menu. Default catches everyone else.
          • Pages: 30 pages, mostly Q1–Q4 2024–2026 Promotions. LTO-reactive, not role-mapped.

          Root causes

          • No role-page routing — one homepage for all users
          • No Learning Plan structure — courses exist without path hierarchy
          • No catalog governance — catalogs created without policy or ownership
          • Channels never activated — enablement layer entirely absent
          • Menu architecture has role gaps — Crew, SL, and AD/RDO fall to Default
          • Pages created reactively — no menu assignment model

          14.4 Design System & Homepage Mockup Color palette, typography, component standards, and a simulated GM homepage in the redesigned visual language.
          +

          Research basis

          Nielsen Norman Group (2020): enterprise learners who rate their LMS as "visually clear and organized" complete training at a 34% higher rate than those rating it cluttered. Mayer's Coherence Principle: extraneous visual elements increase cognitive load and reduce retention independent of content quality. The current promotional-first homepage works against the readiness goal architecturally.

          Color Palette

          • #0F1C2E Deep Navy — nav bar, headers, primary text. Authority and structure.
          • #FFC72C Bojangles Gold — CTAs, active states, progress fills only. Never as background. Reserving gold for action signals shifts the register from promotional to professional.
          • #F8F5F0 Warm Cream — page background. Warm white over harsh pure white; reduces eye fatigue.
          • #FFFFFF Card White — card and widget backgrounds against cream page.
          • #1A4F8A Leadership Blue — leadership and development content category color.
          • #1A7A6E Readiness Teal — Channels, reference resources, readiness content.

          Yellow backgrounds signal promotional content, creating cognitive friction in learning contexts (Foerde & Poldrack, 2009). Gold reserved for action states only.

          Typography System

          • Display — Libre Baskerville (or Georgia): Serif, bold. Page titles and hero widget headers. Conveys trust and authority. Apply via Bo University White Label CSS + Google Fonts import.
          • Interface — DM Sans (or Lato): Sans-serif, 300/400/500/700. All body text, labels, nav items, card titles, form fields. High legibility at small sizes on mobile.
          • Data — DM Mono: Monospace, used sparingly. Data readouts, course codes, role tags in dashboards. Creates visual separation between text and quantitative data.

          Override Docebo's default system-sans via Settings → Bo University White Label → Configure branding and look.

          Research basis

          Bersin & Associates' LMS Governance research: organizations without documented governance spend 40–60% more admin time on content management — not from volume, but from duplication, inconsistency, and remediation. The 138-catalog problem is a direct, measurable governance cost.

          Naming Standard

          • Format: [Role]-[Topic]-[Version]
          • Example: GM-LeadershipFoundations-v2
          • Market prefix optional: Core-GM-LeadershipFoundations-v2
          • No spaces. Only approved abbreviations.
          • Owner: T&D. Enforcement: quarterly naming audit.

          Catalog Creation Rules

          • Maximum 12 governed catalogs — no exceptions without T&D Admin approval
          • New catalog requires: role justification, named owner, visibility scope, review cadence
          • Power Users cannot create catalogs (Admin-only permission)
          • Monthly orphan catalog report (catalogs with 0 enrolled users)

          LTO Lifecycle

          • LTO courses archived within 30 days of promo window close
          • Active LTOs live only in LTO-Active catalog + LTO Central channel
          • No standalone LTO-specific catalogs (current state has dozens)
          • Close date set at launch; auto-flag at 30 days post-close

          Page Publication Checklist

          • Named menu assignment (required)
          • Minimum 2 widgets (required)
          • Mobile preview approved (required)
          • Description field completed (required)
          • Review date set — max 6 months out (required)
          • All 30 existing pages audited in Month 1

          User Group Assignment

          • Every user in exactly one role group
          • Default Menu = pending assignment only (not permanent home)
          • Group updated within 5 business days of role change
          • Monthly report: Default Menu users >5 days → T&D alert

          Governance Calendar

          • Monthly: Default Menu users >5 days · Channels past review date · Pages past review date · Courses not accessed in 90+ days
          • Quarterly: Insights review · LTO archive sweep · Skill coverage report · Naming audit
          • Annually: Catalog architecture review · Skill taxonomy review · Full permissions audit
          14.6 Data & Measurement Framework Passive signal collection, role-scoped dashboards, and the QuickSight integration path — all achievable in Docebo Insights.
          +

          Guiding principle

          Instrument what people already do. The LMS generates intelligence passively — from clicks, searches, revisits, and dropouts — rather than relying on surveys or self-report. Every signal below is capturable in Docebo Insights without additional tooling.

          🧭 Navigation Signals

          • Search queries with zero results → content gaps
          • Most-used nav items by menu/market
          • Homepage widget click-through rate
          • Time-to-first-action on login (orientation friction)

          📖 Engagement Signals

          • Course completion % by role and market
          • Content revisit rate (reference value signal)
          • Learning path dropout point by module
          • Channel saves (high-intent reference signal)

          ✅ Verification Signals

          • Observation checklist completion by market
          • Checklist score distribution (observer calibration)
          • Time gap: course complete → observation
          • Skill coverage % by role

          📍 Readiness Signals

          • Path completion % by market (which markets have gaps)
          • New hire path velocity (onboarding speed by market)
          • Leadership path completion vs. promotion milestone timing

          🛠️ Platform Health

          • Users in Default Menu >5 days
          • Courses with no skill mapping
          • Channels past review date
          • Enrollments with no activity after 14 days

          🔗 QuickSight Integration

          • GM Path completion vs. store P&L performance
          • Market readiness score vs. turnover rate (LMS + HRIS join)
          • Training velocity for new GMs vs. time-to-performance targets
